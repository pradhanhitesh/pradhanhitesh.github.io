Connections to Cognition: The influence of social network diversity on brain health and cognition

Background: Poor social connectedness has been associated with an increased risk of dementia. Additionally, the patterns of social relationships largely remain population and culture-specific. Little is known about the association of social network diversity (ND) with brain and cognitive performance among rural Indians.

Methods: We analysed the cross-sectional data from the Centre for Brain Research - Srinivaspura Aging, Neuro Senescence and COGnition (CBR-SANSCOG) study, collected from January 2018-December 2023 (N = 1115). Subjects aged 45+ years underwent clinical, cognitive and neuroimaging assessments. We excluded subjects with cognitive impairment (Clinical Dementia Rating scale, CDR ≥ 0.5) and abnormal brain pathology. ND was assessed with Cohen’s Social Networking Index questionnaire, and categorized into limited and diverse group based on the median cutoff. Cognitive performance was assessed across attention, memory, language, and visuospatial domains using a culturally-adapted version of the Computerized assessment of adult information processing (COGNITO) battery. Test scores were standardized, and the global cognitive score was calculated as the weighted-average of standardized test scores. Subjects underwent MRI brain scan on 3T Siemens Prisma. Voxel-based morphometry examined volumetric differences in the brain at family-wise error corrected p < 0.05. Multiple linear regression analysis assessed the association between ND and cognitive performance adjusting for age, sex, education, diabetes, hypertension, depression, smoking and alcohol status, and BMI. We also investigated the moderation effect of ND on the association between brain volume and cognition.

Results: In our study population (56.33±8.52 years, 63% females), higher ND was associated with better global (β = 0.31, 95% CI [0.16, 0.46], p < 0.001) and domain-specific cognitive performance (Table 1). Larger brain volumes were observed in temporal pole, left insula, hippocampus and amygdala in individuals with higher ND (Figure 1). Additionally, ND moderated the association between brain volumes and global cognitive performance (β=-0.52, 95% CI [-0.94, -0.10], p=0.015).

Conclusions: Promoting network diversity among rural Indians could be a cost-effective community level strategy to prevent cognitive decline and dementia risk.
